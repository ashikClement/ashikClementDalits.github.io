Healthcare Disparities Between The General Population And Dalits

scroll to start reading

The issue

The life expectancy of Dalit men is 63.5 years, compared to 67.1 years for general category men and the life expectancy of Dalit women is 65.9 years, compared to 69.7 years for general category women.

The cause

Social determinants of health

Dalits are more likely to live in poverty, which is a major social determinant of health. According to a 2021 study by the World Bank, 21.6% of Dalits in India live below the national poverty line, compared to 10.8% of the general population.* Poverty can lead to several health problems, such as malnutrition, infectious diseases, and chronic diseases.
Dalits are also more likely to experience discrimination, which can also have a negative impact on health. A 2022 study by the Indian Institute of Public Health found that Dalits are more likely to be denied healthcare services than the general population.* Discrimination can also lead to stress and social isolation, which can also have a negative impact on health.

Access to healthcare

Dalits are less likely to have access to quality healthcare services. This is due to several factors, including financial barriers, lack of awareness of government healthcare schemes, and discrimination from healthcare providers.
A 2021 study by the National Health Mission found that only 57.6% of Dalit children are fully immunized, compared to 68.2% of general category children.* This suggests that Dalit children are less likely to receive essential vaccinations, which can put them at risk for infectious diseases.
A 2022 study by Oxfam India found that 50% of Dalits faced difficulties in accessing non-COVID medical facilities, compared to 18.2% of the general population.* This suggests that Dalits are more likely to face financial and other barriers to accessing healthcare services.

Nutritional status

Dalits are more likely to be malnourished than the general population. A 2020 study by the National Nutrition Mission found that the stunting rate among children from Dalit households was 40.2%, compared to 35.3% among children from general category households.* Malnutrition can lead to several health problems, including stunting, anaemia, and impaired cognitive development.
A 2021 study by the Indian Council of Medical Research found that the prevalence of anaemia among Dalit women was 61.4%, compared to 58.6% among general category women.* Anaemia can lead to fatigue, shortness of breath, and decreased productivity.

Exposure to environmental hazards

Dalits are more likely to live in areas with poor sanitation and environmental pollution. A 2022 study by the Centre for Science and Environment found that Dalits are more likely to live in slums, which often have poor sanitation and are more exposed to air and water pollution.*
Exposure to environmental hazards can lead to several health problems, including infectious diseases, respiratory problems, and cancer. A 2021 study by the Indian Institute of Public Health found that Dalits are more likely to die from respiratory diseases than the general population.*

Lifestyle factors

Dalits are more likely to engage in unhealthy lifestyle behaviours, such as smoking and tobacco use. A 2020 study by the Global Adult Tobacco Survey found that the prevalence of tobacco use among Dalits was 38.2%, compared to 22.2% among the general population.* Unhealthy lifestyle behaviours can lead to several health problems, including cancer, heart disease, and stroke.

Solutions to combat this issue

Mobile health clinics

Mobile health clinics have been shown to improve access to healthcare services for Dalits and other marginalized groups. A 2022 study by the Indian Institute of Public Health found that a mobile health clinic program in rural India led to a significant increase in the number of Dalits who received preventive healthcare services.

Nutrition programs

Nutrition programs have been shown to improve the nutritional status of Dalit children and women. A 2021 study by the National Health Mission found that a nutrition program in India led to a significant decrease in the stunting rate among Dalit children.

Sanitation projects

Sanitation projects have been shown to reduce the exposure of Dalits to environmental hazards. A 2022 study by the Centre for Science and Environment found that a sanitation project in India led to a significant decrease in the prevalence of diarrhoea among Dalit children.

Education

Education about healthcare and healthy lifestyle behaviours can help to reduce health disparities. A 2020 study by the Indian Council of Medical Research found that an educational program in India led to a significant increase in the number of Dalits who adopted healthy lifestyle behaviours, such as eating healthy foods and exercising regularly.

How you, a citizen of India, can help combat this issue

Volunteer at a health clinic that serves Dalits. You can help to provide medical care, educate patients about preventive health, or assist with administrative tasks.
Donate to a charity that is working to improve the health of Dalits. You can support a variety of programs, such as mobile health clinics, nutrition programs, and sanitation projects.
Support Dalit-led organizations. These organizations are working to empower Dalits and advocate for their rights.
Speak out against discrimination. If you see someone being discriminated against, speak up and say something. You can also educate others about the importance of equality and inclusion.
Promote healthy lifestyles in your community. You can encourage people to eat healthy foods, exercise regularly, and avoid smoking and tobacco use.
Health disparities between the general population and Dalits in India are a complex issue with no easy solutions, but we can all play a role in creating a more equitable and just society by working together to address the root causes of these disparities.
